Why £20/week isn't just two extra hours work for many Universal Credit claimants

30th September 2021

As of 6th October, the government is removing the extra £20/week that was added to Universal Credit payments during the pandemic. On 13th September, the Work and Pensions Secretary, Thérèse Coffey claimed on BBC Breakfast that this £20 could be made up by claimants through finding an extra two hours of work each week. But due to the way Universal Credit works, this claim is misleading and in many cases will not be true. Scroll down to find out why.

Update: since publishing this post, the government has changed the taper rate for Universal Credit from 63% to 55%, meaning less income is withdrawn as a claimant's hours increase. The calculations in this post were based on the previous 63% rate.

We can imagine someone claiming Universal Credit who is trying to work extra hours to make up the lost £20, and plot how much they would earn against how many extra hours they worked, assuming they are working at the National Minimum Wage of £8.91/hour.

We can first just look at the raw extra pay they would receive...

...if we do this, we see that they would need to work 2.25 extra hours to get £20 raw extra pay...

...however, this doesn't take into account the Universal Credit taper - for each pound earned, a claimants benefit amount is reduced by 63p, unless they are eligible for a work allowance (we'll get to that in a moment)...

...if we take into account the taper, it is actually 6.07 extra hours that they would need to work in order to make up the £20.

We also need to account for tax - if the claimant earns more than £184/week (roughly 21 hours at the minimum wage) then they'll be subject to 12% National Insurance payments and if they earn more than £242/week (roughly 27 hours at the minimum wage) then they'll be subject to 20% income tax. This then needs to be applied before we take off 63% for the taper...

...so if the claimant is subject to tax, we see that they actually need to work an extra 8.92 hours per week in order to get back the lost £20.

However, there is also the Work Allowance - this is an amount that some claimants can earn before the taper kicks in. To be eligible, you must be responsible for a child or have a long-term illness or diability that effects your ability to work. This allowance is £293 per month for someone who has a housing cost element as part of their payment, and £515 for those who don't...

...so if we take an example where someone was previously not working and now started working (and who has the housing element cost element), we see that they would need to work 2.25 hours to earn the £20 they lost from the Universal Credit reduction. This is the closest we come to Thérèse Coffey's claim.

However, the claimant may well have already been working some hours, meaning that extra hours could take them over their work allowance limit...

...for example, someone who was already working 7 hours/week would quickly exceed the work allowance limit if they took on extra hours, which would result in the taper kicking in. In this case, we see that they would need to work 3.99 hours to make back the lost £20.

So we can see that there are many ways in which a claimant may need to work significantly more than just 2 hours per week in order to earn the £20 that is being cut from Universal Credit. Only for a particular subset of individuals - those who are eligible for the Work Allowance and still have more than two hours of that allowance left - will it be possible to take home the full amount of earnings that they would receive from extra hours of work. We don't have figures on how many people fall into that group. The latest data on the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) Stat Xplore portal indicates that 39.9% of people on Universal Credit are in employment, meaning that, if they are eligible for the Work Allowance, they will already have used at least some of it. However, firstly, the proportion of claimants in employment may well differ when looking at those eligible for the Work Allowance, and secondly, this does not tell us about the number of hours worked.

Nonetheless, the picture is clearly more complicated than £20 being something that all claimants could make up by simply working an extra two hours. What's more, we also need to consider the needs of those who are unable to increase their hours, be that due to disability, caring responsabilities or other barriers to employment.
